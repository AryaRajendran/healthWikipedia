 In phonology, syncope (; Greek: syn- + koptein “to strike, cut off”) is the loss of one or more sounds from the interior of a word, especially the loss of an unstressed vowel. It is found both in synchronic analysis of languages and diachronics . Synchronic analysis is an analysis that views linguistic phenomena only at one point in time. Usually, the present. We find syncope happening within the functioning of modern languages. Here we consider it in three areas : Inflection, Poetry, and Informal Speech. In languages such as Irish, the process of inflection can precipitate syncope. Historically in old Irish, as a rule, syncope happened whenever the addition of an ending gave rise to syncope. Sounds may be removed from the interior of a word as a rhetorical or poetic device, whether for embellishment or for the sake of the meter. Various sorts of colloquial reductions might be called "syncope". It is also called compression.
