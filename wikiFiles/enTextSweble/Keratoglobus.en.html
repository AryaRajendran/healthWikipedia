It causes corneal thinning, primarily at the margins, resulting in a spherical, slightly enlarged eye. It is sometimes equated with "megalocornea". It is a much rarer condition than keratoconus, which is the most common dystrophy of the cornea. Similar to keratoconus it is typically diagnosed in the patient's adolescent years and attains its most severe state in the twenties and thirties. Keratoglobus is a little-understood disease with an uncertain cause, and its progression following diagnosis is unpredictable. If afflicting both eyes, the deterioration in vision can affect the patient's ability to drive a car or read normal print. It does not however lead to blindness per se. Keratoglobus continues to be a somewhat mysterious disease, but it can be successfully managed with a variety of clinical and surgical techniques. The patient is at risk for globe perforation because the thinned out cornea is extremely weak. Because of the similarities with keratoconus (degenerative deformation and thinning of the cornea), the same conservative surgery could be used in early stages in order to reinforce the cornea. Methods include Intrastromal corneal ring segments, Mini Asymmetric Radial Keratotomy (M.A.R.K.) and Cross-linking. Further progression of the disease usually leads to a need for corneal transplantation because of extreme thinning of the cornea. Primarily, large size penetrating keratoplasty has been advocated.
